Employee Insight Report

GFP │ Somdal Integration

Spencer McCoy │ Interview by Elliott Culp │ May 2026

Spencer McCoy

Current Position: Head of Production (informal) │ Somdal — Shreveport Office
Tenure / Background: Approximately 14 years with the firm; began as a college intern, transitioned to full-time around 2014–2015. Role has been built and expanded over time rather than formally defined.

What Do You Know / Have You Heard About the Planned Combination?

Spencer has received only high-level communication from Chris. He knows the merger with GFP is happening and has been told it will begin coming together in early-to-mid summer. Beyond that, he has very little detail: “Not a whole lot, to be honest.” Key operational questions — how production will move forward, how the teams will integrate, what changes in practice — remain unanswered on his end.

What Are Your Thoughts About the Plan?

His outlook is cautiously positive. He acknowledges it has been framed in a positive light and he does not oppose it. However, he is operating with significant open questions: Does Somdal maintain its autonomy in Shreveport? Does his day-to-day work change? Going from roughly 11 people to potentially 35 raises real logistical questions. “Do we maintain our autonomy up here, or does everybody integrate into one company? So a lot of questions, I guess, to make it really work.”

Before Hearing of the Plan, How Did You See the Next 5–10 Years?

His plan was straightforward. He assumed Chris would eventually transition toward retirement — Chris is approximately 55 — and that over the next eight to ten years Spencer would likely buy into a partnership or ownership role. “There's not many more levels above me to go.” He was open to that path and saw it as a natural progression given his tenure and scope of responsibility.

How, If At All, Has That Changed Since Learning of It?

The merger is a concern — not a dealbreaker, but a complicating factor. “It certainly makes things a little more complicated and certainly dilutes that partnership level a little bit. You've got a lot of other people at play now.” He was candid that this thought has crossed his mind. The ambiguity around what the ownership pathway looks like in a 35-person combined firm is a live question with no current answer.

 

 

 

 

Describe Your Role / How Do You Spend Your Time

Spencer self-describes his role as Head of Production, though the title is informal and only developed over the last 18 months. His own preferred description: “I just get shit done around here.”

In practice, his day is a constant mix of:

Overseeing production staff — ensuring drawings are getting out, everyone is moving toward milestones

§  Consultant coordination — initiating relationships, setting up kickoffs, then handing off day-to-day communication to project managers

§  Scheduling — building and maintaining project timelines, tracking upcoming milestones with Chris in a weekly meeting

§  Quality control — day-to-day informal checks plus formal milestone red-line reviews

§  Direct project work — roughly one-third of his time, often late evenings when daytime hours are fragmented

§  Ad hoc support — a large, untracked portion of his day goes to pop-in questions, clarifications, and “10 minutes here, 10 minutes there” that never get logged

He acknowledged that his actual productive hours are front- and back-loaded, because the middle of the day gets consumed by the people around him. “A lot of time I'll clock those hours, but my kind of midday hours get lost a lot of times.”

What Is Your Ideal State / Magic Wand Scenario?

Spencer's ideal role is a natural step up from where he is — more managerial, less hands-on production. Specifically:

§  More time managing schedules, overseeing staff, and working with consultants

§  Less time on direct drawing/production (the current ~1/3 could decrease somewhat)

§  Continued involvement with people — he enjoys helping everyone and mixing across projects

§  Not 100% production, and not 100% anything else: “I think just my knowledge… I've definitely got a lot more to offer than just that.”

He described this as “a pretty typical role of architects once they get to this kind of level of experience” — a sentiment that reflects both self-awareness and a reasonable expectation that the firm should be actively working toward this with him.

Quality Control

Spencer is the sole person performing formal QC in the Shreveport office. His QC process has two layers:

1.       Milestone reviews — Spencer builds the project schedules and milestone timelines. As each milestone is hit, staff submit work to him and he marks it up with red lines. Peer reviews between production staff also occur at these points. These formal reviews represent roughly half a day per week, or about 10% of his time.

2.       Day-to-day quality — Constant, informal checks throughout the day — “is this wrong, is this right, is this what this would look like” — these are not tracked or logged but represent a significant ongoing time investment.

In Spencer's absence, Chris would be the next person someone would bring QC concerns to. There is currently no backup QC structure, no second reviewer, and no documented QC process beyond what Spencer carries in his head and in the schedules he builds.

Tell Me About the Process: Job In to Job Out

1.       Intake — Virtually all work comes in through Chris. He goes out and gets jobs.

2.       Weekly scheduling meeting — Spencer and Chris meet weekly to review project status, upcoming milestones, and who is doing what.

3.       New project evaluation — When something new comes in, Spencer and Chris assess: Who has capacity? Who has relevant experience? Does anything need to be reshuffled?

4.      Assignment — A project manager is assigned. One to two people typically work a project at any given time. The PM tends to “own” their project from that point forward.

5.       Scheduling — Spencer builds a project schedule immediately, estimating duration and fitting it into the production calendar.

6.      Production — Drawing and design work proceeds; Spencer remains in a quality and oversight capacity.

7.       Consultant coordination — Spencer typically sets up and initiates consultant relationships (structural engineers, etc.), then hands off day-to-day communication to the PM. Consultant interaction by the PM is described as “probably not enough.”

8.      Milestone reviews — At defined milestones, work comes back to Spencer for QC review and red-line markups.

9.      Delivery — Work is assembled and issued to the owner/client.

Spencer and Chris maintain a shared scheduling tool and project calendar. By the time Chris brings a project in, they essentially take it — “If we can make money on it, we'll do it.”

What Do You Like About the Process / Strongest Part?

Flexibility. Spencer described the firm's biggest strength as its ability to adapt on the fly — shuffling people between projects, reassigning when someone better-suited becomes available, and moving fast without bureaucratic friction. “We've never had a rigid structure, and it's just — it works for us.” In a 10-person office where you can open a door and holler at somebody, this informality is genuinely efficient.

He also noted that project-to-person matching is handled thoughtfully — when assigning work, both experience and availability are considered, and he believes the office does a good job not mismatching talent to projects.

What Would You Change About the Process / Weakest Part?

Internal communication. Spencer's single biggest improvement priority is getting everyone on the same page: “I've been trying to get everybody to just communicate better so everybody knows what everybody else is working on.” The recurring pain point is that Chris will work one-on-one with a staff member on something, and Spencer — as Head of Production — won't find out until after the fact. “They've got a kind of side deal going on. It's just like, I just want to know.”

Beyond that, the entire operation runs on fire-drill mode: “Everything's always fire drilling. That's just the standard mode of operation here.” Last-minute deadlines, reactive scheduling, and unplanned rework are not exceptions — they are the norm. Spencer didn't frame this as a crisis, more as an accepted reality. But when pressed on what one thing he'd change, it was communication first.

Concerns

§  Ownership pathway — The clearest and most personal concern. Spencer had a reasonable and long-held expectation of buying into a partnership role as Chris moved toward retirement. The merger introduces new partners, new people, and a lot more complexity into that path. He has not been told what happens to that trajectory.

§  Role clarity in the combined firm — Does he manage production for both offices? Does he now have multiple chains of command? “Do I have eight bosses now? What happens?”

§  Company identity — The basic question of what this firm becomes — does Somdal become GFP? Does it get a new name? — remains unanswered for him and his colleagues.

§  Engagement and productivity — He was receptive to the framing that when people sit in uncertainty, they tighten up and put their heads down. That risk is live here.

Questions Spencer Had for Elliott

§  “Is this a good merger deal? Is it beneficial for everybody?”

§  “What does this look like in 8–10 years if it takes off — 40 people, growing more, or does it just maintain?”

§  “Are we looking to eventually be acquired by somebody bigger, or is the idea for this to be self-sustaining and for people like me to be able to buy in?”

Elliott answered directly: the plan is not a PE exit or a larger acquisition. The goal is a self-feeding ownership structure where people who grow into leadership roles can buy in. Two paths were outlined — either leading a team/division or taking on a defined administrative/operational ownership role. Spencer responded positively: “That certainly sounds positive and gives us something to grow and look forward to.”

Notes & Observations

Spencer is grounded, self-aware, and straightforward. He does not complain, does not dramatize, and does not have a hidden agenda. He is exactly what he says he is: the person who makes sure things get done. His lack of a formal title does not seem to bother him operationally — he thrives in ambiguity and has built his authority through competency, not hierarchy.

However, the absence of a formal role creates real organizational risk in two ways: (1) the firm is operationally dependent on one person who carries QC, scheduling, production oversight, and consultant management with no documented backup, and (2) Spencer has a reasonable expectation of upward mobility that the merger is now complicating without any direct acknowledgment or plan to address it.

He is not currently disgruntled. He is in a “wait and see” mode. That window will not remain open indefinitely.

Recommendation

Formalize Spencer's role immediately — title, scope, and a written career pathway anchored to the partnership/buy-in structure Elliott described. This should happen early in the integration process, not after everything else is sorted. Spencer has done the work for 14 years; telling him what his future looks like is not a reward, it is a retention necessity.

The QC function needs to be documented, expanded, and made less dependent on one person. As the firm scales to 35+, Spencer's current informal approach — even if highly effective — cannot be the entire system.

Spencer is a strong candidate for a cross-firm production leadership role. His appetite for managing people, his institutional knowledge, and his openness to a more defined managerial identity make him well-suited to bridge offices operationally.
